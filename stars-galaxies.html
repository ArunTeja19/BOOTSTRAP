The Stellar Life Cycle

This is the longest stage of a star's life (like our Sun), where it fuses hydrogen into helium in its core. Star color and size depend heavily on their initial mass.

Example: Our Sun

After exhausting core hydrogen, the star expands massively. Low-to-medium mass stars become Red Giants; massive stars become Red Supergiants, capable of fusing heavier elements.

Example: Betelgeuse

Low-mass stars shed their outer layers (Planetary Nebula) leaving a White Dwarf. High-mass stars end in a spectacular Supernova, leaving behind a Neutron Star or, if massive enough, a Black Hole.

Example: Crab Nebula Pulsar

Galactic Morphology

Spiral Galaxies

A spiral galaxy is a beautiful, flattened, rotating disk of stars, gas, and dust with distinctive spiral arms curving out from a bright central bulge. These arms are active sites of new star formation, filled with young, hot stars, while the bulge and halo contain older stars. They are categorized by arm tightness (Sa, Sb, Sc) and often have a central bar (barred spiral), making them common and visually striking galaxies in the universe.

Characterized by a rotating disc of stars, gas, and dust, and a central bulge. They are often classified into barred and unbarred spirals. Most younger, active galaxies fall into this category.

Spiral galaxies are usually found in low-density regions of the universe, meaning they tend to isolate and not exist in the center of galaxy clusters.

Examples: The Milky Way, Andromeda (M31).

Elliptical Galaxies

These range from nearly spherical to highly flattened shapes. They contain very little dust and gas and are typically composed of older, redder stars. They often result from the merger of two or more spiral galaxies.

Classified by their degree of flattening (E0 for circular to E7 for very stretched), they represent the most massive systems in the universe, forming from massive galaxy mergers or intense starbursts and evolving passively with very little change today, making them "red and dead" systems.

Examples: M87, ESO 325-G004.

Irregular Galaxies

These do not fit into the spiral or elliptical categories. They are often chaotic in appearance, lack a clear structure, and are rich in gas and dust, indicating active star formation.

Irregular galaxies are with no defined shape, lacking the spiral arms or elliptical symmetry of other types, often appearing chaotic due to gravitational interactions like collisions or near-misses with larger galaxies, rich in gas, dust, and ongoing star formation, making them crucial for understanding early galaxy evolution.

Examples: Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC), Small Magellanic Cloud (SMC).
