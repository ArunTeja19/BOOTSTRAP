Detailed Vehicle Technical Profiles

Starship / Super Heavy

The fundamental shift in space transport. This vehicle is designed not just for orbit, but for permanent Mars colonization, supporting over 100 crew members per flight. Starship represents a complete re-think of space logistics.

Key Systems:
  • Engines: 33 x Raptor on Super Heavy Booster
  • Propellant: Subcooled Methane/LOX
  • Crew Capacity: Up to 100+
  • Historical Context: Fully Private Development
Core Metrics
  • Class: Super Heavy Lift
  • Payload LEO: 150,000 kg
  • Total Height: 120m
  • First Flight: 2021 (Uncrewed Test)

Space Launch System (SLS)

The world's most powerful operational rocket, designed specifically for the Orion crew capsule to execute the high-stakes missions of the Artemis lunar program. It represents the pinnacle of government space engineering.

Key Systems:
  • Engines: **4 x RS-25 (Shuttle Heritage)**
  • Boosters: **2 x Solid Rocket Boosters**
  • Crew Vehicle: **Orion MPCV**
  • Historical Context: **Derived from Space Shuttle tech**
Core Metrics
  • Class: Heavy Lift
  • Payload LEO: 105,000 kg
  • Total Height: 111m
  • First Flight: 2022 (Artemis I)

Falcon 9: Propulsion Schematic

The Merlin engine series is the backbone of the Falcon 9, balancing performance with reusability. The nine engines offer critical "engine out" capability for enhanced safety. The grid fins are key to controlled atmospheric re-entry.

Merlin 1D Performance:
  • Thrust (Sea Level): **845 kN**
  • Propellant: **Densified LOX / RP-1**
  • Re-ignition Capable: **Yes (Vacuum Version)**
  • Control Mechanism: **Grid Fins for Re-entry**
Core Metrics
  • Class: Medium Lift
  • Payload LEO: 22,800 kg
  • Total Height: 70m
  • Status: Active Service

The Future of Heavy-Lift

The current landscape of launch vehicles is defined by two major trends: extreme reusability (pioneered by Starship and Falcon 9) and maximum payload capacity (led by SLS). The shift from expendable rockets to reusable systems represents the greatest cost reduction opportunity in space history. The next decade will focus on maturing these technologies and securing high-frequency launch availability for deep space infrastructure, lunar gateways, and eventual Mars transit habitats.
